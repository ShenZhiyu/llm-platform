# Underwater Sound Propagation Notes

Source references:
- NOAA Ocean Service, Ocean Facts: https://oceanservice.noaa.gov/facts/sound.html
- NOAA Ocean Explorer education materials on sound in the sea: https://oceanexplorer.noaa.gov/

This local knowledge-base note summarizes public educational material about how sound behaves in seawater. It is intended for retrieval testing and operational question answering in this prototype.

Sound travels efficiently through seawater because water is much denser than air and supports pressure waves with relatively low absorption at many frequencies. The actual speed of sound in the ocean varies with temperature, salinity, and pressure. Temperature usually dominates near the surface, while pressure becomes increasingly important with depth.

A sound-speed profile describes how sound speed changes from the surface to deeper water. When the profile bends acoustic paths back toward a depth layer, sound energy can become trapped and travel farther than it would in a uniform medium. A surface duct can support propagation near the sea surface, while deeper sound channels can support long-distance propagation under favorable conditions.

Sharp gradients, including thermoclines, can refract acoustic energy and create shadow zones where direct acoustic paths are weak. This is why sonar detection range depends not only on source level and receiver sensitivity, but also on local oceanographic structure.

For a knowledge-base answer, cite this document when the question asks about sound-speed profiles, refraction, surface ducts, deep sound channels, thermoclines, or sonar detection-range variation.
